## What You’ll Learn

### Vehicle Control Problem (VCP) — plain terms

You control two numbers every tick: **steering** (wheel angle) and **longitudinal command** (throttle/brake).  
The goal is to follow a path **quickly** and **safely**. We’ll show how simple rules create predictable behaviour—and where they fail.

### Core ideas you’ll recognise when you see them

- **Preview / Lookahead** — aim ahead, not at your feet. Too small → twitchy; too large → lazy.  
- **Curvature → Speed** — tighter bend ⇒ lower feasible speed (friction limit).  
- **Gain Scheduling** — high speed demands softer steering gains.  
- **Oscillations & Noise** — why “just steer to the checkpoint” chatters.  
- **Smoothness vs Lap Time** — jerk/command-rate trade-offs.

### From lane-keeping to racing

We start with **“don’t leave the lane,”** then layer speed planning and style.  
Racing adds: optimizing progress, cutting apexes intelligently, and living near the friction limit.

### Two controller mindsets

- **Style-Parameterized Controller** — a small, interpretable set of knobs
  (**apex offset**, **entry/exit bias**, **risk**, **smoothness**).
  Turn a knob → predict the effect.
- **MPCC (foil)** — powerful optimal control with weights and constraints.
  Great results; **style is implicit**.

### Challenge mode: race the ghost

Tune style parameters and try to beat two baselines:
a **conservative preview controller** and an **MPCC-like ghost**.
Same track, same start. Your job: pick parameters that fit the corner sequence.

### Why this matters beyond racing

The same ideas show up in **autopilot**, **mobile robots**, and **self-driving**:
preview, curvature-aware speed, rate limits, and trade-offs between comfort, safety, and time.

## Setting the Stage

### Inputs you command
- **Steering**: \(\delta\)  
- **Longitudinal**: \(a_x\) (throttle/brake)

### State you observe (assumed known)
- Position \((x,y)\), heading \(\psi\), speed \(v\)  
- A **reference path** (centerline or waypoints)

### Task (formal)
Map \((x,y,\psi,v,path) \rightarrow (\delta, a_x)\) to complete a lap.
We use a lightweight kinematic model and **friction-limited** speed targets:
clear cause → effect without heavy math.

## Why the naive idea fails

**“Point at the next checkpoint and turn toward it.”**  
Looks sensible. In tight bends it **chatters**, misses apexes, and ignores that tighter curvature demands **lower speed**.

## The five ideas we’ll use

1. **Preview (Lookahead)** — pick a target ahead along the path.  
2. **Curvature → Target Speed** — set v_t(s) ≈ √(μ g / |κ(s)|) and smooth it.
3. **Gain Scheduling** — reduce steering gain at higher speeds.  
4. **Smoothness** — limit jerk/command-rate (low-pass on \(\delta\), \(a_x\) or rate penalties).  
5. **Explicit Style** — expose **apex**, **entry/exit bias**, **risk**, **smoothness** as interpretable knobs.
